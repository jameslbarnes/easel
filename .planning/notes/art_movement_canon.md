# Art Movement Canon — Visual Fingerprints for Shader Design

Top 3 artists × top 5 works per movement. Fingerprints are visual not narrative — front-loaded with palette, edge style, technique, and formal device that suggest a GPU operation.

Sources: MoMA, Tate, Met, Wikipedia collections (linked per movement).

---

## 1. ART NOUVEAU (c.1890–1910)

**Visual signature:** Long whiplash curves, asymmetric organic linework, gilded ornament, muted vegetal palette of moss-green / mauve / ochre, flat figure on patterned ground.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Domain-warped sine field driving thick variable-width strokes with gold-leaf bloom; flat colour fill bounded by smooth signed-distance contours.

Refs: https://www.metmuseum.org/toah/hd/anou/hd_anou.htm · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/a/art-nouveau

### Artist 1 — Alphonse Mucha (1860–1939)
1. **Gismonda** (1894) — Tall narrow format, Byzantine halo arc behind figure, muted earth palette with gold accents, every contour a slow elastic curve.
2. **The Four Seasons** (1896) — Decorative panel set; figures bounded by flowering arches, pastel flat fills, repeating floral border like a tiled texture.
3. **Job Cigarette Poster** (1896) — Cascading hair rendered as concentric whiplash curves, gold filigree ground, single saturated red against cream.
4. **Zodiac** (1896) — Circular halo of zodiac symbols behind profile, dense radial ornament, hair flowing as continuous curve fields.
5. **F. Champenois Imprimeur-Éditeur** (1897) — Reclining figure on patterned tile ground, peach and sage palette, hair as sweeping calligraphic loops.

### Artist 2 — Gustav Klimt (1862–1918)
1. **The Kiss** (1907–08) — Couple as silhouette inside a single gold rectangle, mosaic of square and oval ornaments, flat figure-ground, no shadow.
2. **Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I** (1907) — Face floats in a sea of beaten gold, eye triangles and spiral motifs as repeating tiles, almost no atmospheric depth.
3. **Judith and the Head of Holofernes** (1901) — Gold-leaf ground with embossed pattern, skin in pale ivory, dense decorative borders compress space.
4. **Tree of Life** (1909) — Spiralling branches as parallel whiplash curves on gold, dense field of birds and triangles, ornament as wallpaper texture.
5. **Danaë** (1907–08) — Figure curled into a circle, saturated gold hair as cascading rivulets, body flat against patterned ground.

### Artist 3 — Aubrey Beardsley (1872–1898)
1. **The Peacock Skirt** (1893) — Pure black-on-white linework, no halftone, peacock feather curves as nested arcs, vast empty negative space.
2. **The Climax** (1893) — Flat black silhouettes against white, blood as a single curling thread, extreme economy of line.
3. **Salomé title page** (1894) — Asymmetric balance, candle-flame curves, pure two-tone, ornament floating in white field.
4. **The Black Cape** (1894) — Single huge black mass dominates the frame, edges as serpentine curves, whitespace is form.
5. **How Sir Tristram Drank of the Love Drink** (1893) — Dense black foliage as repeating teardrop shapes, white figures cut from black, perfectly flat.

## 2. FAUVISM (1905–1908)

**Visual signature:** Pure unmixed pigment straight from tube, complementary clashes (orange/violet, red/green), thick visible brushstrokes, no atmospheric perspective, colour as emotion not description.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Posterize to ~6 hues at maxed saturation, then displace by short directional brush-noise; complementary-colour edge boost.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/learn/moma_learns/themes/fauvism · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/f/fauvism

### Artist 1 — Henri Matisse (1869–1954)
1. **Woman with a Hat** (1905) — Face in green, violet, and orange patches, no skin tone preserved, brushy unblended strokes.
2. **The Open Window, Collioure** (1905) — Pink walls, green shutters, magenta sea, every plane a different saturated hue, no shading.
3. **The Joy of Life** (1905–06) — Flat curving fields of orange, pink, and yellow, figures outlined in single colour, Arcadia as colour-block landscape.
4. **Portrait of Madame Matisse / The Green Line** (1905) — Vertical green stripe down the centre of the face dividing pink and ochre halves.
5. **The Dance (I)** (1909) — Five vermillion figures on flat green ground under flat blue sky, three-colour composition, no modelling.

### Artist 2 — André Derain (1880–1954)
1. **Charing Cross Bridge** (1906) — Thames in pink and yellow, sky in green, bridge as red strokes, pointillist-derived broken touches.
2. **The Turning Road, L'Estaque** (1906) — Trees as flat orange and green vertical bands, road as cobalt curve, figures as colour silhouettes.
3. **Mountains at Collioure** (1905) — Pure colour dabs separated by white canvas showing through, mosaic-like, divisionist palette pushed to fauve saturation.
4. **Big Ben** (1906) — Tower in violet, sky in lemon and rose, Thames in pink, no local colour preserved.
5. **Boats in the Port of Collioure** (1905) — Hulls as red and orange wedges, water as blue and green dashes, sky as bare canvas with pink accents.

### Artist 3 — Maurice de Vlaminck (1876–1958)
1. **The River Seine at Chatou** (1906) — Heavy impasto in cadmium red and ultramarine, sky as orange streaks, water as broken violet ribbons.
2. **Restaurant de la Machine at Bougival** (1905) — Building in flat scarlet against green foliage, brushwork thick and directional.
3. **Tugboat on the Seine, Chatou** (1906) — Boat as red wedge, water in green and blue parallel strokes, sky in pink streaks.
4. **Houses at Chatou** (1905) — Saturated red rooftops against viridian trees, complementary clash, no shadow.
5. **The Bridge at Chatou** (1907) — Hot-cool dialectic: orange bridge over violet water, brush direction as form.

## 3. EXPRESSIONISM / DIE BRÜCKE (1905–1913)

**Visual signature:** Acid-saturated colour, woodcut-derived jagged contours, distorted anatomy, harsh black outlines, primitive carving feel, raw and unblended.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** High-contrast posterize with carved-wood noise overlay, black ridge contours from edge detect, slight skew/shear distortion of form.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/learn/moma_learns/themes/german-expressionism · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/d/die-brucke

### Artist 1 — Ernst Ludwig Kirchner (1880–1938)
1. **Street, Berlin** (1913) — Acid pinks and greens, figures as elongated splinters, aggressive diagonal perspective, faces masklike.
2. **Marzella** (1909–10) — Child portrait in flat orange, green, and blue, jagged outline, face as carved planes.
3. **Self-Portrait as a Soldier** (1915) — Severed-hand figure in violet uniform on yellow ground, palette knife scraping visible, raw edge.
4. **Five Women in the Street** (1913) — Tall figures in green-black coats against pink pavement, almost abstract verticals, faces as triangular masks.
5. **Bathers at Moritzburg** (1909) — Orange bodies on green grass, blue water, woodcut-jagged contours, no modelling.

### Artist 2 — Emil Nolde (1867–1956)
1. **The Last Supper** (1909) — Faces in burning orange and yellow, packed shoulder-to-shoulder, eyes as black voids, paint laid thick and wet.
2. **Dance Around the Golden Calf** (1910) — Figures in scarlet and yellow against violet, gestural sweep, brushwork as choreography.
3. **Masks** (1911) — Five grotesque masks tiled flat, each a different saturated colour, black eye-holes punching through.
4. **Tropical Sun** (1914) — Disc of flat orange, sea in jagged horizontals of cobalt and green, sky in magenta bands.
5. **Prophet** (1912 woodcut) — Black-on-cream woodcut, gouged white lines through a black face, raw chisel marks.

### Artist 3 — Erich Heckel (1883–1970)
1. **Fränzi Reclining** (1910) — Nude body in pink and green patches, harsh black contour, woodcut feel transferred to oil.
2. **Two Men at a Table** (1912) — Compressed interior, figures with green and yellow faces, room walls in clashing red.
3. **Day of Glass** (1913) — Faceted landscape in fractured cobalt and viridian, woman in foreground as angular flat planes.
4. **Crouching Woman** (1913 woodcut) — Black-and-white woodcut, body cut as faceted black masses, knife marks as visible texture.
5. **Bathers in Reeds** (1909) — Yellow nudes in green water, every form bounded by chisel-jagged outline.

## 4. CUBISM — ANALYTIC PHASE (1908–1912)

**Visual signature:** Monochrome ochre / grey / umber palette, object shattered into overlapping faceted planes, multiple viewpoints in one frame, shallow space, no single light source.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Voronoi tessellation with per-cell affine transform of source image; desaturate to ochre-grey; multiplicative overlap of fragmented planes.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/learn/moma_learns/themes/cubism · https://www.metmuseum.org/toah/hd/cube/hd_cube.htm

### Artist 1 — Pablo Picasso (1881–1973)
1. **Portrait of Daniel-Henry Kahnweiler** (1910) — Face dissolved into translucent grey-ochre facets, only eye and watch chain readable, near-monochrome.
2. **Ma Jolie** (1911–12) — Vertical stack of brown facets with stencilled letters and treble clef, near-abstract grid.
3. **The Accordionist** (1911) — Pyramid of fractured umber planes, instrument keys as repeating motif, figure barely emergent.
4. **Girl with a Mandolin (Fanny Tellier)** (1910) — Figure as semi-transparent overlapping rectangles, ochre and grey, shallow relief.
5. **Still Life with Chair Caning** (1912) — Oval canvas with collaged oilcloth chair-caning pattern, rope frame, fragmented letters JOU.

### Artist 2 — Georges Braque (1882–1963)
1. **Violin and Candlestick** (1910) — Instrument exploded into grey-brown facets on table, near-monochrome, shallow stacked planes.
2. **The Portuguese** (1911) — Stencilled letters and numbers floating over a faceted figure, brown-grey scaffold, deep flatness.
3. **Houses at L'Estaque** (1908) — Cubes of buff and olive tessellating up the canvas, no perspective, geometry as landscape.
4. **Man with a Guitar** (1911–12) — Body as grid of overlapping translucent planes in ochre, instrument fragments scattered through.
5. **The Castle at La Roche-Guyon** (1909) — Architecture as stacked beige and green prisms, sky compressed flat against form.

### Artist 3 — Juan Gris (1887–1927)
1. **Portrait of Picasso** (1912) — Face built from cool blue-grey diamond facets, more rigorous geometry than Picasso/Braque, repeating tile feel.
2. **The Sunblind** (1914) — Newspaper, glass, and blind as overlapping flat patterned rectangles, near-collage.
3. **Still Life with Checkered Tablecloth** (1915) — Sharp diagonal grid, objects as flat colour shards, palette clean.
4. **Guitar and Pipe** (1913) — Instrument as nested flat shapes, wood-grain pattern as texture sample, faux-bois trompe-l'oeil.
5. **The Smoker** (1913) — Head as crystalline flat polygons, pipe as single line, pipe smoke as repeating curl.

## 5. FUTURISM (1909–1916)

**Visual signature:** Force lines radiating from motion, repeated phantom outlines (chronophotography traces), interpenetrating planes, electric blues / hot reds, dynamism over object.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Frame-echo / motion-blur trail with directional smear; radial speed lines via polar gradient; multi-pass copies offset along velocity vector.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/learn/moma_learns/themes/futurism · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/f/futurism

### Artist 1 — Umberto Boccioni (1882–1916)
1. **The City Rises** (1910) — Horses and workers dissolved into red-orange streaks, brushwork as vector field, divisionist dabs at high speed.
2. **States of Mind: The Farewells** (1911) — Steam, numbers, and figures fused in spiralling green and violet curves, force-lines as composition.
3. **Dynamism of a Cyclist** (1913) — Bicycle and rider shattered into faceted blue and orange wedges, motion erases the object.
4. **Unique Forms of Continuity in Space** (1913, sculpture) — Striding figure with flame-like flanges trailing behind it, body as wake.
5. **Elasticity** (1912) — Horse and rider as overlapping translucent planes in green and red, every contour repeated four times.

### Artist 2 — Giacomo Balla (1871–1958)
1. **Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash** (1912) — Dog's legs and tail multiplied into ~20 phantom positions, chronophotographic copy-paste.
2. **Abstract Speed + Sound** (1913–14) — Curved bands of sky-blue, green, and pink sweeping diagonally, no figure, motion as pure colour vector.
3. **Girl Running on a Balcony** (1912) — Figure rendered in pointillist dabs and repeated outlines, like motion-blurred film.
4. **Street Light** (1909) — Lamp emitting V-shaped chevrons of complementary colour, light as repeating arrow patterns.
5. **Iridescent Interpenetration No. 7** (1912) — Field of nested coloured triangles tiling the canvas, pure abstract pattern.

### Artist 3 — Gino Severini (1883–1966)
1. **Dynamic Hieroglyphic of the Bal Tabarin** (1912) — Dancers as scattered sequins of colour with collaged sequins, chaotic confetti composition.
2. **Suburban Train Arriving in Paris** (1915) — Locomotive shattered into wedges of green and grey, smoke as repeating arcs.
3. **Armoured Train in Action** (1915) — Train top-down, soldiers as identical repeated rectangles, rifles as parallel diagonals.
4. **Blue Dancer** (1912) — Figure dissolved into pointillist dabs and sequins, body as cloud of colour particles.
5. **Sea = Dancer** (1914) — Spiralling curves and fractured planes with collaged sequins, sea and figure interchangeable.

## 6. CONSTRUCTIVISM / SUPREMATISM (1915–1925)

**Visual signature:** Pure geometric primitives — circles, rectangles, diagonals — in red / black / white / occasional yellow, sans-serif Cyrillic typography integrated, sharp edges, no texture, revolutionary poster energy.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Hard-edge boolean SDF composition (rect ∪ circle ∪ rotated bar); 3-colour palette clamp; angular Suprematist diagonals as rotated half-plane masks.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/80385 · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/c/constructivism

### Artist 1 — Kazimir Malevich (1879–1935)
1. **Black Square** (1915) — Black square on white field, painted edges visible, the irreducible icon.
2. **White on White** (1918) — White tilted square on white square ground, edges as faint tonal shifts only.
3. **Suprematist Composition: Airplane Flying** (1915) — Black, red, and yellow rectangles drifting at angles on white, weightless geometry.
4. **Black Circle** (1915) — Single black disc on white, slightly off-centre.
5. **Suprematist Painting (Eight Red Rectangles)** (1915) — Eight identical red bars at parallel diagonals, rhythm by repetition.

### Artist 2 — El Lissitzky (1890–1941)
1. **Beat the Whites with the Red Wedge** (1919) — Red triangle piercing white circle on black field, pure poster geometry.
2. **Proun 19D** (1922) — Axonometric stack of black, ochre, and red rectangles floating in grey, architecture as abstraction.
3. **Proun 1A, Bridge I** (1919) — Diagonal black bars and red square, no perspective horizon, geometry as construction.
4. **Self-Portrait (The Constructor)** (1924, photomontage) — Hand holding compass overlaid on grid paper, double exposure.
5. **For the Voice** (1923, book design) — Red and black sans-serif typography arranged as geometric blocks, type as image.

### Artist 3 — Alexander Rodchenko (1891–1956)
1. **Books! In all branches of knowledge** (1924, poster) — Cropped photo of woman shouting, red and black geometric blocks, sans-serif type as architecture.
2. **Spatial Construction No. 12** (1920) — Concentric plywood ovals suspended in space, hung sculpture casting linear shadows.
3. **Pure Red Color, Pure Yellow Color, Pure Blue Color** (1921) — Triptych of three flat monochrome canvases, primary colour as endgame.
4. **Lengiz: Books in All Branches of Knowledge** (1925) — Same red-and-black photomontage poster style, diagonal rule, type at 90°.
5. **Stairs** (1929, photograph) — Extreme high-angle, woman climbing stairs as repeating diagonal stripes, geometric photograph.

## 7. DADA (1916–1924)

**Visual signature:** Photomontage chaos, found-object collage, typewriter and newsprint typography exploded across the page, mixed scales, deliberate ugliness, anti-aesthetic.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Random-cut texture atlas with per-shard rotation and scale jitter; channel-split chromatic abberation; halftone print noise overlay.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/collection/terms/dada · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/d/dada

### Artist 1 — Hannah Höch (1889–1978)
1. **Cut with the Kitchen Knife Dada Through the Last Weimar Beer-Belly Cultural Epoch of Germany** (1919) — Dense newsprint photomontage, hundreds of clipped faces and machine parts, no single focal point.
2. **The Beautiful Girl** (1920) — Female figure assembled from advertising cutouts, lightbulb head, BMW logo, mechanical parts.
3. **Dada-Ernst** (1920–21) — Layered cutouts of legs, gears, and text, off-register, brown-cream palette of newsprint paper.
4. **From an Ethnographic Museum** (1929) — Tribal masks fused to modern bodies, surreal collage hybrids on flat ground.
5. **Indian Dancer (From an Ethnographic Museum)** (1930) — Photomontage portrait, eyes replaced with cutout knives, deliberately wrong scale.

### Artist 2 — Marcel Duchamp (1887–1968)
1. **Fountain** (1917) — Porcelain urinal signed "R. Mutt", readymade as anti-art (visual: smooth white ceramic curve, isolated).
2. **L.H.O.O.Q.** (1919) — Mona Lisa reproduction with pencilled moustache and goatee, defacement as gesture.
3. **Bicycle Wheel** (1913) — Wheel mounted upside-down on stool, two found objects bolted, kinetic readymade.
4. **The Bride Stripped Bare by Her Bachelors, Even (Large Glass)** (1915–23) — Painted dust and lead wire on glass, mechanical diagram aesthetic, transparent two-panel sandwich.
5. **Nude Descending a Staircase No. 2** (1912) — Mechanical figure in chronophotographic copies cascading diagonally, ochre-brown palette.

### Artist 3 — Raoul Hausmann (1886–1971)
1. **Mechanical Head (The Spirit of Our Time)** (1920) — Wooden mannequin head with ruler, watch parts, and crocodile wallet glued on.
2. **The Art Critic** (1919–20) — Photomontage with giant pencil, banknote eyes, German banknote and shoe-shop receipt as ground.
3. **ABCD** (1923–24) — Self-portrait collage with letterforms erupting from open mouth, typography as scream.
4. **Tatlin at Home** (1920) — Constructivist artist's head fused with steamship engine and map fragments.
5. **Dada Cino** (1920) — Stacked cutout images of legs, faces, and text in chaotic vertical column, scale-mismatched layers.

## 8. DE STIJL (1917–1931)

**Visual signature:** Rectangular grid of black orthogonal bars dividing a white field, fills strictly red / blue / yellow primaries plus white / black / grey, no curves, no diagonals, no texture.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Axis-aligned BSP partition with primary-only colour assignment; pure flat fills, anti-aliased only at sub-pixel grid edges.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/collection/terms/de-stijl · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/d/de-stijl

### Artist 1 — Piet Mondrian (1872–1944)
1. **Composition with Red, Blue, and Yellow** (1930) — Black grid lines on white, three small primary fills, the canonical example.
2. **Broadway Boogie Woogie** (1942–43) — Manhattan grid in yellow, red, blue, and grey segments, no black bars, syncopated rhythm.
3. **Tableau I** (1921) — Large white squares balanced against red, yellow, blue, grey, and black fields, bars of unequal weight.
4. **Composition with Yellow, Blue and Red** (1937–42) — Asymmetric black grid weighted right, single yellow square top-left, sparse.
5. **Victory Boogie Woogie** (1942–44, unfinished) — Diamond canvas, rotated 45°, grid as multicolour staccato squares.

### Artist 2 — Theo van Doesburg (1883–1931)
1. **Composition VII (The Three Graces)** (1917) — Black grid on white with primary fills, looser than Mondrian.
2. **Counter-Composition V** (1924) — Diagonal black bars on white with primary insets, the heretical 45° rotation that broke with Mondrian.
3. **Arithmetic Composition** (1929–30) — Five black squares descending in size diagonally on white, mathematical sequence.
4. **Composition XIII** (1918) — Off-white grid with red, yellow, and blue tiles in irregular rhythm, looser.
5. **Counter-Composition VI** (1925) — Diagonal black rectangles on white with one yellow and one red insert.

### Artist 3 — Bart van der Leck (1876–1958)
1. **Composition 1918 No. 5** (1918) — Black bars and primary triangles on white, more figural ghosting than Mondrian.
2. **The Storm** (1916) — Figures abstracted into red, black, and blue rectangular blocks on white, near-pictogram.
3. **Mine Triptych** (1916) — Workers as flat coloured rectangles in primary palette, figures as ideograms.
4. **Composition** (1917) — Scattered short black bars and primary triangles drifting on white, minimal balance.
5. **Geometric Composition I** (1918) — Pure rectangles in red, yellow, blue floating in white, no black grid.

## 9. BAUHAUS (1919–1933)

**Visual signature:** Geometric primitives (circle / square / triangle) in primary palette plus black, sans-serif Universal-style typography, photographic-grid layouts, industrial clarity, function as form.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** SDF library of primitives composed via union/intersection on a strict grid; weighted-circle gradient fields; 3-primary palette with subtle tonal value steps.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/collection/terms/bauhaus · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/b/bauhaus

### Artist 1 — Wassily Kandinsky (1866–1944)
1. **Composition VIII** (1923) — Sharp triangles, perfect circles, chequerboards, and bars on cream ground, geometry as music.
2. **Several Circles** (1926) — Floating overlapping translucent discs of varied colour against dark ground, soft halos.
3. **Yellow-Red-Blue** (1925) — Yellow square left, blue circle right, red wedges between, primary diagram.
4. **On White II** (1923) — Black diagonals, triangles, and circles on white field, hard-edged geometric drama.
5. **Black and Violet** (1923) — Sharp angular shapes orbiting concentric circles, primary spots, deep black bar anchoring.

### Artist 2 — Paul Klee (1879–1940)
1. **Castle and Sun** (1928) — Tessellated grid of coloured squares forming castle silhouette, single red sun disc, mosaic.
2. **Senecio** (1922) — Face built from soft-edged coloured rectangles in pink and ochre, eyes as unequal circles.
3. **Highways and Byways** (1929) — Receding grid of pink, green, and ochre trapezoids forming road perspective, near-pixel art.
4. **Red Balloon** (1922) — Floating geometric shapes — square house, red disc — on washed coloured ground, dreamy primitives.
5. **Ad Parnassum** (1932) — Pointillist coloured dots forming pyramid silhouette, mosaic-of-dots technique.

### Artist 3 — László Moholy-Nagy (1895–1946)
1. **A II (Construction A II)** (1924) — Overlapping coloured rectangles and discs on grey, transparent layering, planes floating in space.
2. **Q1 Suprematistic** (1923) — Strict primary geometry with diagonal red bar, Suprematist debt.
3. **Composition Z VIII** (1924) — Cross of red bar over black bar with yellow circle, primary geometry rotated.
4. **Photogram** (1922 onward) — Cameraless white silhouettes on black, ghosted geometric objects, light as drawing tool.
5. **Light Prop for an Electric Stage / Light-Space Modulator** (1922–30) — Kinetic chrome sculpture casting moving geometric shadows.

## 10. SURREALISM (1924–1966)

**Visual signature:** Photoreal rendering of impossible juxtapositions, deep illusionistic space, sharp shadows under hard light, dream-bleached palette of cobalt sky / ochre desert / flesh tone, biomorphic forms.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Photoreal sample-and-displace: warp recognisable imagery via curl-noise into biomorphic flow; long hard shadow projection; chromatic dream-tint pass.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/learn/moma_learns/themes/surrealism · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/s/surrealism

### Artist 1 — Salvador Dalí (1904–1989)
1. **The Persistence of Memory** (1931) — Melting watches draped over branch and biomorphic blob, flat ochre desert, hard cobalt sky, raked shadow.
2. **The Elephants** (1948) — Stick-legged elephants on infinite tan plain, hard low light casting long shadows.
3. **The Temptation of St. Anthony** (1946) — Stilt-legged horses and elephants stretched vertical, vanishing-point desert.
4. **Swans Reflecting Elephants** (1937) — Mirrored lake turns swans into elephants on inversion, double-image trick.
5. **Galatea of the Spheres** (1952) — Face dissolved into floating spheres, particle decomposition of portrait.

### Artist 2 — René Magritte (1898–1967)
1. **The Son of Man** (1964) — Bowler-hat man with floating green apple over face, plain blue-grey sky, dead-flat lighting.
2. **The Treachery of Images** (1929) — Photoreal pipe with caption "Ceci n'est pas une pipe", flat cream ground, copperplate brown.
3. **Golconda** (1953) — Identical bowler-hat men suspended in grid against pale sky, repetition as composition.
4. **The Lovers** (1928) — Two heads kissing through cloth shrouds, plain background, claustrophobic.
5. **Empire of Light** (1953–54) — Daytime sky over night-time street with single lamppost, paradox of light, photorealistically rendered.

### Artist 3 — Max Ernst (1891–1976)
1. **The Elephant Celebes** (1921) — Mechanical elephant-boiler creature on bare plain, smoke rising, illusionistic sky.
2. **The Robing of the Bride** (1940) — Owl-headed bride in red feathered cloak, photoreal nude, claustrophobic chamber.
3. **Europe After the Rain II** (1940–42) — Decalcomania-textured ruined landscape, biomorphic decay, pressed-paint texture.
4. **The Forest** (1927) — Frottage-derived dense bark texture forming alien forest, monochrome rust-orange.
5. **Two Children Are Threatened by a Nightingale** (1924) — Painted box with collaged figures and 3D gate, mixed-media depth.

## 11. ABSTRACT EXPRESSIONISM — GESTURE / POLLOCK (1947–1956)

**Visual signature:** All-over drip lacework, no focal point, layered enamel skeins in black / white / aluminium / occasional cadmium, no figure, no horizon, scale immense, surface as record of body motion.

**TouchDesigner-style GPU equivalent:** Particle advection with curl-noise field rendered as layered ribbons; multi-pass overdraw for skein density; black/white/silver palette clamp.

Refs: https://www.moma.org/collection/terms/abstract-expressionism · https://www.tate.org.uk/art/art-terms/a/abstract-expressionism

### Artist 1 — Jackson Pollock (1912–1956)
1. **Number 1A, 1948** (1948) — All-over black, white, and aluminium drip skeins on raw canvas, handprints at top edge.
2. **Autumn Rhythm (Number 30)** (1950) — Black, brown, and white drip lattice, scale 17 ft wide, no centre.
3. **Lavender Mist (Number 1, 1950)** (1950) — Drip web in white, black, and pale rose, soft hazy palette overall.
4. **Blue Poles (Number 11, 1952)** (1952) — Drip ground with eight vertical blue I-beam stripes punctuating, more structured.
5. **Convergence** (1952) — Dense black and primary drip lacework, edges as wild as the centre.

### Artist 2 — Willem de Kooning (1904–1997)
1. **Woman I** (1950–52) — Aggressive scraped figure with bared teeth, slashed pink and yellow flesh, palette-knife violence.
2. **Excavation** (1950) — All-over field of bone-white shards and fleshy curves in cream and black, dense gestural.
